DELHI

HISTORY

Early History

Delhi has been inhabited since at least the 6th century BC, with references found in ancient texts. The earliest known city was Indraprastha, linked to the Mahabharata.

Medieval Period

In the 12th century, Delhi became prominent under the Delhi Sultanate, with rulers like Qutb al-Din Aibak and Iltutmish establishing it as a significant power. The Qutub Minar, a UNESCO World Heritage site, was built during this time.

Mughal Era

The Mughals made Delhi their capital in the 16th century. Shah Jahan, known for building the Red Fort and Jama Masjid, transformed the city into a cultural and architectural hub.

Colonial Period

In the 19th century, after the Indian Rebellion of 1857, the British shifted the capital from Kolkata to Delhi in 1911. They constructed several new buildings, including the Raisina Hill area, which is now home to the Rashtrapati Bhavan.

Independence and Modern Era

After India gained independence in 1947, Delhi became the national capital of the Republic of India. Since then, it has grown into a major metropolitan area, blending historical sites with modern development.

Today, Delhi stands as a symbol of India's historical legacy and its dynamic present, representing a fusion of diverse cultures and traditions.

BEST TIME TO VISIT

The ideal time to visit Delhi is from October to March. During these months, the weather is pleasant, with temperatures ranging from 10°C to 20°C (50°F to 68°F), making it comfortable for sightseeing and outdoor activities.

October marks the beginning of autumn, leading to clearer skies and milder temperatures, perfect for exploring historical sites and vibrant markets.

December to February brings cooler temperatures, which can occasionally drop to single digits at night. This is also the season for various cultural festivals and events, enhancing the experience of visiting the city.

It’s advisable to avoid the extreme heat of summer (April to June) and the monsoon season (July to September) for a more enjoyable visit.

PLACES TO EXPLORE

1. Red Fort

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, this iconic fort symbolizes India's rich history and architecture. Visitors can explore its stunning gardens and museums.

2. Qutub Minar

The tallest brick minaret in the world, the Qutub Minar is surrounded by historical structures, showcasing Indo-Islamic architecture.

3. India Gate

This war memorial is a popular gathering spot, especially in the evenings, with beautiful surrounding gardens and fountains.

4. Humayun's Tomb

An exquisite example of Mughal architecture, this tomb is a precursor to the Taj Mahal and set in beautiful gardens.

5. Lotus Temple

Famed for its unique lotus-shaped structure, this Bahá'í House of Worship is open to people of all faiths, offering a serene atmosphere for meditation.

6. Chandni Chowk

A bustling market area known for its vibrant streets, traditional shops, and delicious street food, offering a taste of old Delhi's charm.

These attractions reflect the diverse history and culture of Delhi, making it a fascinating city to explore.

WHERE UNITY MEETS DIVERSITY!

LANGUAGE AND CULTURE

Delhi is a melting pot of languages and cultures, reflecting its diverse population. The primary languages spoken are Hindi and English, with Punjabi, Urdu, and Bengali also widely used due to the city's multicultural demographic.

The culture of Delhi is a rich tapestry woven from various influences, including Mughal, British, and regional traditions. This is evident in its festivals, cuisine, music, and dance.

Festivals such as Diwali, Eid, Christmas, and Lohri are celebrated with enthusiasm, showcasing the city's communal harmony.

Delhi's culinary scene is equally diverse, offering everything from street food like chaat and kebabs to fine dining that features regional cuisines from across India.

The arts thrive in Delhi, with numerous theaters, galleries, and cultural institutions that promote local and international talent, making it a vibrant cultural hub.

FAMOUS DISHES

Delhi is renowned for its diverse and flavorful cuisine, which reflects its rich cultural heritage. Here are some must-try dishes:

1. Chaat: A popular street food, chaat includes a variety of savory snacks like aloo chaat, papri chaat, and dahi puri, known for their tangy and spicy flavors.

2. Butter Chicken: A rich and creamy dish made with marinated chicken cooked in a tomato-based sauce, often enjoyed with naan or rice.

3. Biryani: Delhi's biryani, often made with fragrant basmati rice and marinated meat, is a flavorful one-pot dish that has many regional variations.

4. Kebabs: From seekh to shammi kebabs, Delhi offers a wide range of succulent grilled meats that are marinated with spices and cooked to perfection.

5. Paratha: A stuffed flatbread, often filled with potatoes, paneer, or other vegetables, typically served with pickles and yogurt.

These dishes highlight the culinary diversity of Delhi, making it a paradise for food lovers.

FESTIVALS

Delhi is a vibrant city that celebrates a variety of festivals throughout the year, reflecting its rich cultural diversity. Here are some of the prominent festivals:

  • Diwali: The Festival of Lights, celebrated with enthusiasm, where the city is adorned with lights, and families burst crackers and exchange sweets.
  • Holi: The Festival of Colors, celebrated with exuberance as people throw colored powders and water, symbolizing the arrival of spring.
  • Eid: Significant for the Muslim community, involving communal prayers, feasting, and sharing sweets with friends and family.
  • Christmas: Celebrated with decorations, carols, and gift exchanges, creating a festive atmosphere throughout the city.
  • Dussehra: Marking the victory of Lord Rama over Ravana, celebrated with processions and the burning of effigies.

    • These festivals foster communal harmony and showcase the rich cultural tapestry of Delhi, making it a lively and colorful place to experience traditions.
